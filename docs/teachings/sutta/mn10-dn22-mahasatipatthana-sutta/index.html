[Mindfulness of the Body]

‚ÄúMonks, how does a monk abide observing the body as the body?

[Mindfulness of Breathing]

‚ÄúMonks, a monk who has gone to the forest, to the base of a tree, or to an empty building sits down, crosses his legs, sets his body upright, and establishes mindfulness as foremost4. One breathes in mindfully, one breathes out mindfully.

‚ÄúBreathing in a long breath, one understands, ‚ÄòI am breathing in a long breath.‚Äô

‚ÄúBreathing out a long breath, one understands, ‚ÄòI am breathing out a long breath.‚Äô

‚ÄúBreathing in a short breath, one understands, ‚ÄòI am breathing in a short breath.‚Äô

‚ÄúBreathing out a short breath, one understands, ‚ÄòI am breathing out a short breath.‚Äô

‚ÄúOne trains in this way: ‚ÄòI will breathe in experiencing the whole body.‚Äô

‚ÄúOne trains in this way: ‚ÄòI will breathe out experiencing the whole body.‚Äô

‚ÄúOne trains in this way: ‚ÄòI will breathe out calming the body.‚Äô

‚ÄúIn this way, one abides observing the body as the body internally, or one abides observing the body as the body externally, or one abides observing the body as the body both internally and externally. [Complete Awareness]

‚ÄúMonks, just as a skilled butcher or apprentice butcher, after killing a cow and slicing it into pieces, might sit at a crossroads, in the same way, monks, a monk considers this body, however it is positioned or directed, as composed of elements: ‚ÄòIn this body there is the earth-element, the water-element, the heat-element, the wind-element.‚Äô

‚ÄúIn this way, one abides… observing the body as the body.

‚ÄúMonks, just as if a monk were to see a corpse discarded in a cemetery that was being eaten by crows, hawks, vultures, dogs, jackals, and worms, he would compare it to this body: ‚ÄòThis body is also of that nature, it is becoming like that, it is not exempt from that.‚Äô In this way, one abides… observing the body as the body.

[Mindfulness of Phenomena]

‚ÄúMonks, how does a monk abide observing phenomena as phenomena?

[Mindfulness of the Five Components]

‚ÄúMonks, a monk abides observing phenomena as phenomena in relation to the five components10 when affected by attachment11. Monks, how does a monk abide observing phenomena as phenomena in relation to the five components when affected by attachment?

‚ÄúMonks, a monk knows the body, the manifestation of the body, and the disappearance of the body; he knows feelings, the manifestation of feelings, and the disappearance of feelings; he knows recognition, the manifestation of recognition, and the disappearance of recognition; he knows mental constructs12, the manifestation of mental constructs, and the disappearance of mental constructs; he knows consciousness, the manifestation of consciousness, and the disappearance of consciousness.

[Mindfulness of the Four Noble Truths]

‚ÄúMonks, a monk abides observing phenomena as phenomena in relation to the four noble truths. Monks, how does a monk abide observing phenomena as phenomena in relation to the four noble truths?

‚ÄúMonks, a monk understands suffering15 as it is; he understands the cause of suffering as it is; he understands the cessation of suffering as it is; he understands the practice which leads to the cessation of suffering as it is.

[Suffering]

‚ÄúMonks, what is the noble truth of suffering? Birth is suffering, decrepitude is suffering, dieing is suffering; sorrow, grief, pain, depression, and anguish are suffering; involvement with what one dislikes is suffering; separation from what one likes is suffering; not getting what one wants is suffering; briefly, the five components [of existence] when affected by attachment are suffering.

‚ÄúMonks, what is birth? The birth, nascence, entry, production of beings in a category of being16; the manifestation of the components; the acquisition of the sense-domains ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòbirth.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is decrepitude? The decrepitude, agedness, fragility, greyness, wrinkledness of beings in a category of being; the diminution of vitality; the decay of faculties ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòdecrepitude.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is dieing? The departure, falling away, breaking, disappearance, death, dieing of those beings from that category of being; separation from the components [of existence]; leaving behind the corpse; interruption of vitality ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòdieing.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is sorrow? The sorrow, sorrowing, sorrowfulness, inner sorrow, thorough inner sorrow of one who has been touched by some kind of disaster or unpleasant phenomenon ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòsorrow.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is grief? The lament, grief, lamenting, grieving, lamentation, state of grief of one who has been touched by some kind of disaster or unpleasant phenomenon ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äògrief.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is pain? An unpleasant feeling [such as] physical pain, physical discomfort, [or] pain produced by a physical experience ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòpain.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is depression? An unpleasant feeling [such as] mental pain, mental discomfort, pain produced by a mental experience ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòdepression.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is anguish? The torment, anguish, tormented state, state of anguish of one who has been touched by some kind of disaster or unpleasant phenomenon ‚Äì monks, this is called ‚Äòanguish.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what does ‚Äòinvolvement with what one dislikes is suffering‚Äô mean? ‚ÄúMonks, what does ‚Äòseparation from what one likes is suffering‚Äô mean? ‚ÄúMonks, what does ‚Äònot getting what one wants is suffering‚Äô mean?

‚ÄúMonks, a wish like this manifests in beings who are subject to decrepitude… dieing… sorrow, grief, pain, depression, and anguish: ‚ÄòMay we not be subject to sorrow, grief, pain, depression, and anguish! ‚ÄúMonks, what does ‚Äòthe five components [of existence] when affected by attachment are suffering‚Äô mean? ‚ÄúMonks, this is called ‚Äòthe noble truth of suffering.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is right perspective? Monks, it is knowing about suffering, knowing about the cause of suffering, knowing about the cessation of suffering, and knowing about the practice which leads to the cessation of suffering. Monks, this is called ‚Äòright perspective.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is right attitude? The attitude of renunciation, the attitude of non-harming, and the attitude of non-cruelty. Monks, this is called ‚Äòright attitude.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is right speech? Refraining from false speech, refraining from malicious speech, refraining from harsh speech, and refraining from useless speech. Monks, this is called ‚Äòright speech.‚Äô

‚ÄúMonks, what is right effort? ‚ÄúMonks, this is called ‚Äòright concentration.‚Äô
